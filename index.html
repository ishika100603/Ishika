Why does this feel unfinished?

Artificial intelligence did not arrive fully formed. It emerged in fragments — a paper in 1950, a summer workshop in 1956, decades of winters and false starts, then a sudden acceleration that no one fully predicted. We are living inside the chapter that has not been written yet.

This site mirrors that process. Text drifts when you look away. Images settle only when you hold your attention on them. The timeline below is a sketch — accurate in outline, unstable in detail, like memory itself.

1950

The question that started everything

Alan Turing asked a deceptively simple question: "Can machines think?" His imitation game — whether a human could tell a machine from a person through conversation — became the philosophical foundation for an entire field.

He imagined learning machines that could grow beyond their initial programming. Seventy-five years later, we are still answering his question, differently each time.

1956

A name is given

At Dartmouth College, John McCarthy coined the term "artificial intelligence." Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and others gathered for a summer workshop with audacious optimism: human intelligence, they believed, could be precisely described and reproduced in machines.

They were wrong about the timeline. They were right about the ambition.

1957–1969

Neurons on wire

Frank Rosenblatt built the Perceptron — a machine that learned from examples, modeled after biological neurons. The New York Times reported it would soon walk, talk, see, and write. Then Minsky and Papert proved its limitations, and funding dried up. The first AI winter arrived.

1997

Checkmate

IBM's Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov. It was not intelligent in the way humans are — it calculated 200 million positions per second. But it changed the public imagination. Machines could beat us at things we considered uniquely human.

2012

Deep learning awakens

AlexNet won the ImageNet competition by a staggering margin, using deep convolutional neural networks trained on GPUs. The approach that had been dismissed for decades suddenly worked — because we finally had enough data, compute, and refined algorithms at once.

2022 — now

Language becomes interface

ChatGPT reached 100 million users in two months. Large language models turned AI from a specialist tool into something anyone could converse with. The technology is powerful, unreliable, creative, and hallucinatory — often all at once. We are still learning what it is.
